# One latent for the whole robot

*July 2026 · Menlo Research · Kepler-Encoder-v0.1*

At Menlo Research we are building embodied AI, and an embodied model must understand the body it runs on. **Kepler-Encoder-v0.1** is our first step toward that goal, a robot-first multimodal embedding model that fuses vision, proprioception, and force/torque into a single shared latent space, trained self-supervised on 12,776 real robot scenes spanning four embodiments. Because every stream lands in one latent, a downstream model can read the robot's full body state, including the parts outside the camera frame, from a single vector. This post explains why we believe that fusion is necessary, how the encoder works, and what it delivers today.

## The challenge of seeing your own body

A single camera frame carries almost no information about force. We measured this before building anything, and a probe from frozen vision features to held-out force/torque readings scores an R² of roughly zero, which means it does no better than predicting the mean. Contact and torque change the image too little for pixels to reveal them, so a robot that reasons about its body from video alone is missing part of its own state. The natural question is where the rest of that state should come from.

Robot state already flows through most pipelines, and we think it deserves a much larger role. In common practice a vision backbone encodes the camera image while joint values and gripper aperture enter downstream as a short vector of raw numbers. That design descends from internet-scale pretraining, where an image is disembodied and could depict anything, but a robot's camera lives in a very different regime. Which images the camera produces is determined by joint configuration, gripper aperture, and contact, so vision and state describe the same underlying body and each stream can teach the other. This coupling is what pushed us to encode them jointly.

Joint encoding at this scale has to be self-supervised. Annotating what a force reading or a contact event means, at the volume representation learning needs, is impractical, while the raw sensor streams get recorded for free during ordinary operation. Self-supervised learning turns that free data into a training signal, and within the self-supervised family the JEPA line fits robotics especially well, because it optimizes a latent to support predictions about the world, and anticipating the consequences of actions is precisely what a robot needs its representation for. Our starting point is therefore the JEPA objective, adapted to multimodal robot data.

## A multimodal JEPA

The Kepler encoder keeps the JEPA training objective and changes what sits on each side of the prediction. A standard JEPA encodes two views of one signal, two crops of an image for example, and a predictor conditioned on a variable z maps one representation onto the other. In our version, x is the set of visible modalities fused into one latent, y is a hidden modality's target embedding, and the predictor runs without z, because the identity of the masked modality already determines what the target is. Training proceeds by **masked cross-modal latent prediction**. On every pass an attention mask hides one modality, rotating over vision, motor, and end-effector, and the fused latent of the remaining streams must predict the hidden modality's embedding, produced by a slow exponential-moving-average copy of its projection. This recipe respects the information asymmetry between sensors, since vision can never fully determine force, and the objective only asks vision to extract whatever force-relevant cues the pixels do carry. The LeJEPA SIGReg regularizer, applied per modality and on the fused latent, prevents collapse, and we monitor latent health directly with RankMe throughout training. An objective built on heterogeneous token sets then needs an architecture that can ingest them.

## One fuser for every robot

We represent every sensor reading as a token so that a single module can fuse an arbitrary sensor suite. In the instantiation of this report, a frozen LeJEPA ViT-B/16 contributes 196 patch tokens, proprioception fills an 8×3 motor grid, and the end-effector stream fills a 13×15 window of native-rate force/torque and TCP samples, giving 217 context tokens at a shared width of d = 256. Eight learned queries cross-attend over this set through two pre-norm blocks and mean-pool into one latent, which keeps the cost at O(NM) and the output shape constant however many sensors a robot exposes. The whole trainable head comes to roughly 2M parameters.

The vision backbone stays frozen throughout, and we arrived at that choice through experiments. Continuing pretraining on robot video with a hot learning rate collapsed the encoder, with RankMe falling from 300 to 158, while a gentle rate restored latent health yet left every robot-relevant probe flat, and force R² stayed near zero at every checkpoint. Robot video, at this scale, adds nothing robot-relevant to a strong general backbone, so force has to enter the latent through its own modality. Freezing also concentrates all learning in the fusion head, which keeps training cheap and the comparison to vision baselines clean.

A fixed token grid with validity masks is what lets one encoder span different robots. The corpus mixes a 6-joint UR5, 7-joint Flexiv and KUKA arms, and a Franka that lacks a physical force/torque sensor, and every one of them fills the same 8×3 motor grid and 13×15 end-effector window, with unpopulated rows and channels masked. Under this scheme a missing sensor behaves identically to a masked one, so the Franka doubles as a built-in test of missing-modality robustness, and every robot exports a latent of identical shape. With the objective and the architecture in place, the question becomes whether one shared encoder actually holds up across embodiments.

## One encoder, four robots

We designed the evaluation to catch self-deception before it could flatter the model. All training and probing runs on RH20T, a real-robot manipulation corpus of 12,776 scenes across 7 rig configurations and 4 embodiments, with held-out groups keyed by (config, task, user) so that near-duplicate scenes from one user can never straddle the split, and every number averages 5 seeds. Each result pairs a label-free collapse check, RankMe, with an unsaturated robot-relevant probe, and the probed feature is always the vision-only latent with all state masked at evaluation, so a probe can only measure what the encoder learned to read out of pixels. The headline question under this protocol is whether one encoder trained on every robot can cover all of them.

It can. We trained four per-robot specialists and one ALL encoder, probed each on every embodiment's held-out groups, and the resulting 5×4 transfer matrix shows the ALL encoder matching or beating each specialist on that specialist's own robot while sitting at or near the best cell of every column. The specialists degrade sharply away from home, with the Franka specialist falling to R² between 0.07 and 0.15 on other robots and even the strong KUKA specialist reading 0.11 to 0.22 off its own hardware. Shared multimodal training appears to teach structure that transfers across bodies, and a healthy RankMe band of about 165 to 180 out of a 256 ceiling confirms the shared latent stays full-rank. The next question is where fusion helps most.

The largest gains land on the quantities the camera is blind to. On the end-effector probe, covering force/torque and TCP pose, the vision-only latent beats raw frozen-ViT features on every robot that carries a force sensor, by +0.06 on Flexiv, +0.08 on UR5, and +0.04 on KUKA, and in our earlier single-embodiment UR5 study the fused latent reached 0.653 on held-out robot state against 0.516 for raw vision. Training vision alongside force teaches it to surface the force-relevant cues a frame does contain, such as contact configurations and gripper strain, which is exactly the payoff the architecture was built for. What remains is to show the gain comes from the cross-modal signal itself.

Two controls carry that attribution. A PCA-256 compression of the same frozen features stays within 0.01 to 0.04 of raw everywhere, and an identically-sized cross-attention encoder trained on vision alone, with the same data and compute, trails the fused latent by 0.10 to 0.17 on the force probe. Between them the controls rule out dimensionality reduction and in-domain training as explanations, which leaves the presence of the other modalities during training as the operative difference. One cell of the matrix resisted this pattern, and it turned out to be informative.

On KUKA motor state the raw features win, at 0.391 against our 0.321. Instantaneous joint pose is largely visible in a single image, so a single-timestep fusion has little to add on that target, while velocity and motion, the quantities that would reward fusion there, live across frames the current model never compares. We read this cell as an arrow pointing at the temporal extension described below.

## What a frozen Kepler encoder is good for today

The trained encoder gives us a runtime safety monitor with zero additional training. Its per-sample cross-modal prediction error acts as a **surprise signal**, staying low while the reported robot state agrees with what the camera sees and rising when the two diverge, and on held-out UR5 data it separates valid from corrupted states at AUROC 0.90 for out-of-range values and 0.69 for states swapped in from another scene. A robot stack can watch this one scalar to flag sensor faults and state corruption as they happen. Beyond monitoring, the latent itself holds more than first appears.

The latent encodes graded world-state that simple probes undersell. A small MLP decodes motor state from the frozen vision-only latent at R² 0.44 against a linear probe's 0.335, a PCA of the latent within UR5 traces a smooth open-to-closed gripper gradient, and a latent-conditioned diffusion decoder, PixNerd, regenerates recognizable scene layout and arm placement from the 256-d vector while blurring fine texture. Together these show the latent retains the state of the world while shedding pixel-level detail, which is the compression a downstream policy or world model wants. Decoding from a state-only latent recovers the arm's pose and little else, matching what proprioception alone can know.

## What's next

The largest open item is time. Every result above fuses modalities within a single tick, while much of what matters about a robot, velocity, motion, the unfolding of contact, lives across ticks, and the streams arrive at mismatched rates of roughly 10 Hz for vision and 100 Hz for force. Every token already carries its real timestamp, so the planned extension embeds those timestamps with continuous-time features and lets the fuser read each stream at its native rate, and we will test that design head to head against the classical resample-then-convolve pipeline, keeping whichever wins. Two caveats bound today's claims while that work proceeds.

Both caveats are about scope. Same-time cross-modal alignment shows the latent contains cross-modal structure, and a claim about modeling dynamics would additionally require action-conditioned prediction of future latents, which waits on the temporal machinery. The encoder also currently sees one external camera, and multi-view support will arrive framed as cross-view prediction, for the same reason the objective already avoids forcing modalities into agreement, since a wrist camera and a side camera hold genuinely different information. We prefer to state these limits plainly and resolve them in order.
