The Kepler encoder keeps JEPA's predict-in-latent objective but drops z and replaces each encoder's input with fused modalities

Left, the Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture with single-signal views and a conditioned predictor. Right, the Kepler encoder, where x is every visible modality fused through a two-layer stack and y is the hidden modality's EMA-projected embedding.

Side-by-side comparison of JEPA and the Kepler multimodal encoder

How to read it. JEPA (left) encodes two views of one signal, and a predictor conditioned on a latent variable c maps the x representation to a prediction ŝy scored against sy by a distance D. The Kepler encoder (right) keeps that objective but changes what x and y are. Each modality gets its own projection layer, so 196 vision patches, 8 motor rows and 13 end-effector slots become 217 context tokens of width d = 256, and a perceiver with 8 learned queries fuses the visible ones into a single latent z. The predictor is a plain MLP with no conditioning variable, since the masked modality itself defines the target. That target sy is the EMA copy of the hidden modality's projection (shown for motor), and the hidden modality rotates over vision, motor and end-effector on every pass.
