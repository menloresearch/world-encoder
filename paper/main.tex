\begin{abstract}
  Embodied AI models are models that run on a physical body, but they are also models that need to understand that state of their body. In the intersection of robotics and emobodied AI, this body is the robot. In this technical report we present \textbf{Kepler-Encoder-v1}, a robot-first multimodal embedding model that treats robot state as a modality of data and maps vision and robot state into a single shared latent space. This allows the robot to percieve its own body even if it is not within the frame of its cameras. This allows a us to encode useful additional information in our encoder which could be used for downstream applications. To manage the heterogeneous data at their respective native sampling rates, we make use of a Perceiver~IO backbone~\citep{perceiverio2021} cross-attends the resulting heterogeneous token set into a fixed-size latent. The encoder is trained in a self-supervised manner and regularized against collapse by the LeJEPA/SIGReg objective~\citep{lejepa2025}. On the RH20T real-robot corpus~\citep{rh20t2023}, we find that a single encoder trained on all robots matches or beats per-robot specialists on their own robot, and its \emph{vision-only} latent recovers held-out force/torque and joint state substantially better than raw frozen-ViT features. We also show that by measuring the surprise of the multimodal latents at runtime, we are able to identify invalid robot states without training additional classifiers, which are a boon for ensureing runtime safety of the robot. Finally, to demonstrate that visual information remains captured in the latent despite the significant compression along the spatial dimension, we finetune PixNERD~\cite{} to generate the original image from Kepler-Encoder latents and show that reconstruction is possible. We hope that this will provide a reference and direction for further research into better multimodal robot encoders for embodied AI that take advantage of the full information provided by the sensors of a robot.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The problem.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item A robot senses through many heterogeneous channels---RGB video,
        joint encoders, force/torque, gripper state, later audio and IMU---each streaming at a different, irregular native rate (cameras $\sim$7--15~Hz and jittery; force/torque $\sim$100--125~Hz).
  \item Almost every robot-learning system today follows the vision(-language)
        encoder paradigm~\citep{openvla2024}: a vision backbone produces the representation, and robot state is appended downstream as raw numbers. The encoder never sees force, joints, or contact.
  \item Learning a \emph{single} representation that unifies these streams
        across modalities and across embodiments remains open.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why an encoder, and why it must be multimodal.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Everything downstream is bounded by what survives the encoder.}
        If force never enters the latent, no world model or policy built on top can reason about contact. We verify the failure directly: a single video frame does not contain force, so force is essentially unpredictable from vision alone ($R^2 \approx 0$; \S\ref{sec:stage1}).
  \item The encoder is therefore the load-bearing component---and the reusable
        asset: train it once with self-supervision and every downstream task inherits it, evaluated cheaply with linear probes instead of retraining a full policy.
  \item A shared latent also doubles as \emph{compression} for shipping sensor
        data edge~$\rightarrow$~cloud.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why self-supervised, why JEPA, why LeJEPA.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Robot data has no labels at scale, so training must be self-supervised.
  \item \emph{Reconstruction} (autoencoders/MAE) forces the latent to memorize
        pixel-level nuisance (texture, background)---appearance, not world state.
  \item \emph{Contrastive / ``equate''} objectives force different views to be
        \emph{equal}, collapsing the latent to the \emph{intersection} of what all sensors share---but sensors genuinely carry different information (a wrist camera cannot see what a side camera sees).
  \item \emph{JEPA---``predict, don't equate''} masks one part and predicts its
        \emph{latent} from the rest~\citep{ijepa2023}. Prediction respects information asymmetry and is robust to missing sensors by construction (a masked modality at train time $=$ a dead sensor at deployment).
  \item JEPA's failure mode is \emph{collapse}. \textbf{LeJEPA}~\citep{lejepa2025}
        is why we pick this lineage: its SIGReg objective (pushing embeddings toward an isotropic Gaussian along random 1-D slices) provably prevents collapse with almost no tuning, and ships a pretrained ViT checkpoint we warm-start from.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The architectural bet.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Every sensor reading is a token of \emph{(value, what, where, when)}:
        a per-modality tokenizer for the \emph{value}, a learned modality embedding for \emph{what}, spatial position for \emph{where}, and---the core bet---a \textbf{continuous-time embedding of the real timestamp} for \emph{when}.
  \item Encoding \emph{when} as a continuous function of the true timestamp lets
        streams at different native rates fuse \emph{without resampling to a common clock and without zero-padding}---the white space the closest robot-SSL work explicitly avoids (MSDP assumes synchronized observations~\citep{msdp2025}).
  \item A \textbf{Perceiver} fuser cross-attends the whole heterogeneous token
        pile into a fixed-size latent at cost linear in token count, indifferent to how many sensors or which robot---one architecture spanning a 6-DOF UR5 and a 7-DOF KUKA via masking.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A robot-first multimodal encoder} that ingests video,
        proprioception, and force/torque as first-class tokens and fuses them with a Perceiver~IO backbone~\citep{perceiverio2021} (\S\ref{sec:arch}).
  \item \textbf{A cross-modal training recipe} built on LeJEPA: masked
        cross-modal latent prediction plus per-modality and joint SIGReg, with a robot-agnostic state representation that keeps every joint via masking rather than truncation (\S\ref{sec:objective}, \S\ref{sec:tokenization}).
  \item \textbf{Evidence at scale on RH20T} (7 configs, 4 embodiments): a single
        encoder matches per-robot specialists and transfers across embodiments, and the vision-only latent recovers force/torque better than raw ViT features---beating a PCA compression control, isolating the gain as cross-modal (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
  \item \textbf{An evaluation protocol} pairing a label-free collapse detector
        (RankMe), unsaturated robot-relevant probes, and group-level split hygiene, designed to resist single-metric self-deception (\S\ref{sec:setup}).
  \item \textbf{The continuous-time, multi-rate extension} that is the model's
        central bet, specified and de-risked by the single-timestep results (\S\ref{sec:temporal}).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Roadmap.} \S\ref{sec:related} situates the work.
\S\ref{sec:method} details the architecture, objective, and tokenization. \S\ref{sec:data} describes the RH20T corpus and the robot-agnostic data pipeline. \S\ref{sec:experiments} reports the single-timestep results (the vision-only baseline and the full transfer matrix), with the single-embodiment fusion proof-of-concept in Appendix~\ref{sec:stage2}. \S\ref{sec:discussion} states limitations and \S\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.


\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Joint-embedding and predictive representations.}
Joint-embedding predictive architectures learn representations by predicting latent targets rather than reconstructing inputs, avoiding the pixel-memorization trap of autoencoders~\citep{ijepa2023,data2vec2022}. The paradigm has been applied to video~\citep{vjepa22025}, functional brain signals~\citep{jmaefmri2024}, multivariate time series~\citep{mtsjepa2026}, and robotic motor control~\citep{spatiotemporal2024}. Our training objective follows LeJEPA~\citep{lejepa2025}, whose SIGReg regularizer replaces the architectural anti-collapse heuristics (stop-gradient, predictor asymmetry, teacher momentum) with a distributional constraint. On the multimodal side, masked cross-modal prediction appears in M3-JEPA~\citep{m3jepa2024}, 4M~\citep{fourm2023}, MultiMAE~\citep{multimae2022}, and data2vec~\citep{data2vec2022}; we adopt the predict-one-modality-from-the-rest structure but in \emph{latent} space with SIGReg, over robot sensor streams rather than images/text.

\paragraph{Robot multi-sensor representation.}
Vision-language-action models~\citep{openvla2024} produce a vision-centric representation and treat proprioception as an appended input; heterogeneous pre-trained transformers align proprioceptive-visual streams across embodiments~\citep{hpt2024}. A separate line learns tactile and multisensory touch representations~\citep{sparsh2024,sparshx2025,seehearfeel2022,m3l2023}. The closest work, MSDP~\citep{msdp2025}, pre-trains a multi-sensory dynamics model but \emph{assumes synchronized observations}---precisely the multi-rate assumption we set out to remove. Action tokenization for sequence models~\citep{vqvla2025,fast2025,stablefast2025,actioncodec2026,mojito2025} is complementary: it compresses actions for policies, whereas we compress \emph{observations} into a reusable state latent.

\paragraph{Continuous-time and irregular multimodal fusion.}
The \emph{when} slot borrows continuous-time embeddings from the irregular time-series literature: mTAN~\citep{mtan2021}, Time2Vec~\citep{time2vec2019}, SeFT~\citep{seft2019}, and ContiFormer~\citep{contiformer2024}. COPER couples a continuous-time embedding with a Perceiver~\citep{coper2022}, and FuseMoE~\citep{fusemoe2024} and UTDE~\citep{utde2022} fuse irregular multimodal streams---but all in the electronic-health-record domain. We transplant the continuous-time-token idea into robot sensor fusion, where the rate mismatch (100~Hz force vs.\ 10~Hz vision) is the defining difficulty.

\paragraph{Image and video autoencoders; compression.}
Compression-oriented and diffusion-based video autoencoders~\citep{adaptive1dvdae2026,rvmae2026,videoae2601,videoae2604, multimodalcompression2022} and neural codecs repurposed as tokenizers~\citep{biosignaltokenizers2025} inform the (optional) decoder and the compression framing, but not the training signal: reconstruction is deliberately kept out of the objective.

\paragraph{Robot interaction datasets.}
We train on RH20T~\citep{rh20t2023}, and situate our data choices relative to humanoid~\citep{humanoidseveryday2025}, large-scale manipulation~\citep{agibotworld2025}, mobile manipulation~\citep{airoamoma2025}, and contact-rich force-aware collections~\citep{forcevla22026}.

\paragraph{Downstream validation target.}
Auxiliary latent-prediction objectives improve VLA policies when the target encoder is strong: FLARE~\citep{flare2025} predicts the latent of a future observation and shows the target encoder's quality is the deciding factor. This is a natural downstream slot for a multimodal, native-rate encoder such as ours.


\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Design principles}
Three commitments shape every choice below. (i)~\textbf{Encoder-first:} the representation is the product; downstream models consume the latent, so the latent must contain what they will need---force and contact included. (ii)~\textbf{Predict, don't equate:} fusion is learned by cross-modal \emph{prediction}, never by forcing modalities to agree, which respects information asymmetry between sensors and yields missing-sensor robustness for free. (iii)~\textbf{Native rates, no resampling:} time is carried \emph{in the token}, not by interpolating every stream onto a shared grid---the load-bearing idea for multi-rate fusion (built in \S\ref{sec:temporal}; the single-timestep model of this report is its degenerate one-tick case).

\subsection{Token representation: \emph{(value, what, where, when)}}
\label{sec:tokenization}
Every sensor reading is embedded as a token carrying four components (Figure~\ref{fig:token}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{fig_token_anatomy.png}
  \caption{Every sensor reading becomes a token of \emph{(value, what, where, when)}: a per-modality tokenizer (value), a learned modality embedding (what), spatial position (where), and a continuous-time (mTAN) embedding of the real timestamp (when). The \emph{when} slot is what lets streams at different native rates fuse without resampling; in the single-timestep model of this report it is constant.}
  \label{fig:token}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Value---per-modality tokenizers.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Vision.} Patch tokens from a \emph{frozen} LeJEPA ViT-B/16
        (196 patches $\times$ 768-d). The backbone is frozen on the evidence of \S\ref{sec:stage1} (finetuning on robot video degrades the encoder and adds nothing robot-relevant), not by assumption. Only the fusion head trains ($\sim$2M parameters).
  \item \textbf{Proprioception (motor).} A robot-agnostic $8\times3$ grid: rows
        0--6 are joints, row 7 is the gripper. Channels are scale-respecting encodings---$[\sin q, \cos q, \mathrm{symlog}\,\dot q]$ for joints, symlog width for the gripper. Angles use $\sin/\cos$ to remove the $2\pi$ wrap; unbounded quantities use symlog; rotations use the continuous 6-D representation~\citep{rot6d2019}.
  \item \textbf{End-effector (ee).} A $13\times15$ block of native-rate samples
        in the interval $[\text{tick}_k, \text{tick}_{k+1})$: symlog force/torque (6), symlog TCP translation (3), and TCP 6-D rotation (6), zero-padded to a fixed window.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What---modality embedding.} A learned embedding tags each token with
its modality (vision / joints / gripper / force-torque / \ldots), so the fuser can route by source.

\paragraph{Where---spatial position.} Image patch tokens carry their spatial
position; non-spatial state tokens omit it.

\paragraph{When---continuous-time embedding.} Each token carries an embedding of
its \emph{real timestamp}. In the single-timestep model of this report all tokens of a chunk share one tick, so this slot is constant; \S\ref{sec:temporal} activates it. Crucially, \emph{the timestamp is threaded through every token from the outset}, so turning on the temporal axis requires no data- or interface-level rework.

\paragraph{Robot-agnostic state via masking, not truncation.}
Different robots expose different joint counts (6 for UR5, 7 for Flexiv/Franka/ KUKA) and different sensor suites (Franka has no physical force/torque sensor). Rather than defining a per-robot state vector, we fix the $8\times3$ motor grid and $13\times15$ ee block for \emph{all} robots and \emph{mask} the rows/channels a given robot does not populate. This (a) lets one architecture span every embodiment, (b) keeps joints as first-class tokens instead of dropping them, and (c) makes a missing modality identical to a masked one---so Franka's absent force sensor is a built-in missing-modality robustness test rather than a special case.

\subsection{Architecture: the Perceiver fuser}
\label{sec:arch}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{fig_architecture.png}
  \caption{\textbf{The \texttt{MMPerceiver} fuser (single-timestep
  configuration).} Three heterogeneous streams---frozen ViT-B/16 patch tokens, the $8\times3$ motor grid, and the $13\times15$ end-effector window---are each linearly projected to the shared width $d{=}256$ and tagged with modality/position embeddings, forming a $217$-token context set in which every token carries \emph{(value, what, where, when)}. Eight learned queries cross-attend to this set (Perceiver-IO style, depth 2) and are mean-pooled into the fused latent~$z$. Training is \emph{masked cross-modal prediction}: an attention mask hides one modality and $z$ must predict that modality's EMA-target embedding (predict-don't-equate, MSE), regularized by per-modality and joint SIGReg. At evaluation only vision enters (motor/ee masked) and $z_v$ is the exported latent. Modality-colored inputs (vision blue, motor teal, ee orange); grey $=$ frozen backbone, green $=$ Perceiver, purple $=$ latent, red $=$ the LeJEPA objective.}
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Forward pass.} The fuser is a Perceiver-IO cross-attention
block~\citep{perceiverio2021}; Figure~\ref{fig:arch} traces it end to end. The three projected streams are concatenated into a single context set of $N=217$ tokens ($196$ vision $+$ $8$ motor $+$ $13$ ee)---heterogeneous in origin but homogeneous in width ($d=256$). A small set of $M=8$ learned query vectors cross-attends over this set: in each of two depth blocks the queries attend to the context and pass through an FFN (pre-norm residuals), after which they are mean-pooled into a single fixed-size latent $z\in\mathbb{R}^{256}$. Attention masks do double duty---encoding padding/validity \emph{and} the deliberate modality masking of the training objective---so we note the sign convention explicitly to avoid a common bug: \emph{chunk masks mark valid entries as} \texttt{True} \emph{while cross-attention masks mark blocked entries as} \texttt{True}, so one must be inverted before use.

\paragraph{Why a latent-bottleneck Perceiver.} Three properties make the
latent-query Perceiver the right fuser for heterogeneous robot streams, and each is a deliberate choice over a more obvious default (a full self-attention transformer over all tokens, or late concatenation of per-modality encoders). \emph{(i)~Compute decoupled from input size.} Cross-attention from $M$ queries to $N$ tokens costs $O(NM)$, not the $O(N^2)$ of self-attention over the token set~\citep{perceiverio2021}; adding a sensor, more patches, or (in \S\ref{sec:temporal}) more timesteps grows the context linearly and leaves the latent width untouched. \emph{(ii)~A fixed-size latent under variable input cardinality.} Because the output is $M$ pooled queries---not a per-token map---$z$ has the same shape whether a robot exposes 6 or 7 joints, carries a force sensor or not, or (at eval) sees vision alone. This is what lets \emph{masking}, rather than per-robot truncation, carry embodiment-agnosticism (\S\ref{sec:tokenization}): a missing modality is simply extra blocked columns, and the exported $z$ is dimensionally identical across all four embodiments. A fixed-grid, ViT-style encoder cannot offer this; it is the difficulty that pushes HPT to separate per-embodiment ``stems''~\citep{hpt2024}, which our single shared module avoids. \emph{(iii)~Set-structured, order-free fusion.} The context is a \emph{set} of typed tokens---position and modality live in the embeddings, not in a fixed layout---so one block ingests any token-set composition without architectural change, the property that first motivated the Perceiver family for arbitrary modalities~\citep{perceiverio2021}.

\paragraph{Why learned queries and cross-attention---and what it sets up.} The
$M$ queries act as a learned, content-adaptive pooling that summarizes the token set into a compact code, but the choice is also forward-looking. The Perceiver's structure---\emph{learned queries as reference points, cross-attention as attention-to-observations}---is precisely the decomposition Multi-Time Attention Networks use for irregularly sampled time series~\citep{mtan2021}, a correspondence COPER makes explicit by pairing a continuous-time embedding with a Perceiver~\citep{coper2022}. We adopt the same skeleton deliberately so that activating the \emph{when} slot (\S\ref{sec:temporal}) is an \emph{embedding} change, not an \emph{architecture} change: the fuser that pools three modalities at one tick is the same one that will pool observations across native-rate time.

\subsection{Training objective}
\label{sec:objective}
Training combines a cross-modal predictive signal with two anti-collapse regularizers. Let $z$ denote the fused latent and $z^{(m)}$ the fused latent when modality $m$ is held out.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Masked cross-modal latent prediction (the learning signal).}
        Mask one modality (vision, motor, or ee); predict its exponential-moving-average \emph{target} latent from the other two. This is JEPA's predict-don't-equate~\citep{ijepa2023,data2vec2022} applied across modalities---in the spirit of masked multimodal prediction~\citep{m3jepa2024,fourm2023,multimae2022} but carried out in \emph{latent} space: it forces each stream to encode structure relevant to the others (vision learns force-relevant cues \emph{because it is trained to predict force}), avoids intersection-collapse, and is robust to missing modalities by construction. A shared encoder \emph{without} this signal underperforms single-modality baselines---the cross-modal loss is required, not optional.
  \item \textbf{Per-modality SIGReg.} A SIGReg (SlicedEppsPulley) penalty applied
        to each modality's embeddings before fusion. It prevents per-modality collapse and acts as a magnitude standardizer, making modalities commensurate before they are fused (the standardization that naive concatenation of separately-trained encoders skips).
  \item \textbf{Joint SIGReg.} The same penalty on the fused latent $z$, keeping
        it high-rank (expressive) rather than collapsed.
  \item \textbf{Action-conditioned forward prediction (future work).} Same-time
        cross-modal alignment is only correlational; predicting future latents from actions is the causal engine that earns the term ``world model.'' It is gated behind the temporal machinery of \S\ref{sec:temporal} and not used in this report.
\end{enumerate}

The EMA target encoder is a slow copy of the online encoder, the momentum-target mechanism that stabilizes predictive SSL~\citep{data2vec2022}; SIGReg is the \texttt{SlicedEppsPulley} objective from LeJEPA~\citep{lejepa2025}, which replaces the usual anti-collapse heuristics (stop-gradient, predictor asymmetry, teacher momentum) with an explicit distributional constraint---collapse is instead monitored directly with RankMe~\citep{rankme2023}. Only the fusion head and the per-modality projections train; the vision backbone stays frozen.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{fig_loss.png}
  \caption{Masked cross-modal prediction (shown hiding vision; the hidden modality rotates over vision/motor/ee). The online encoder fuses the visible modalities into $z$; a predictor maps $z$ to the masked modality's latent, whose target is that modality's embedding from an EMA (slow) copy of the encoder---JEPA-style predict-the-latent, not pixel reconstruction. The loss is $\mathrm{MSE}(\text{prediction},\text{target})$ plus per-modality and joint SIGReg (LeJEPA), which prevents collapse.}
  \label{fig:loss}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Continuous-time, multi-rate extension}
\label{sec:temporal}
The single-timestep model validates modality fusion; the central architectural hypothesis is fusing streams \emph{across time at their native rates}. The data side already supports it (tick-anchored native-rate windows with per-token timestamps, \S\ref{sec:data}); the remaining work is model-side.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{fig_temporal.png}
  \caption{The continuous-time, multi-rate extension. Streams arrive at different native rates---vision $\sim$10~Hz, proprioception and force $\sim$100~Hz---each sample carrying an mTAN continuous-time embedding of its real timestamp, so the Perceiver fuses them by real time with no resampling to a common clock and no zero-padding. The single-timestep model of this paper is the one-tick special case.}
  \label{fig:temporal}
\end{figure}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Continuous-time embedding (the \emph{when} slot).} We embed each
        token's real timestamp with a Fourier / Time2Vec feature map~\citep{time2vec2019} using fixed, \emph{log-spaced} frequencies, upgradable to learned frequencies (mTAN proper~\citep{mtan2021}) behind the same interface. We deliberately take only mTAN's continuous-time \emph{embedding}, not the whole network: the Perceiver already supplies mTAN's other half (learned queries are the reference points; cross-attention is attention-to-observations), a point COPER makes architecturally explicit~\citep{coper2022}. The make-or-break knob is the frequency band, which must span $\sim$ms (100~Hz force) to seconds (multi-second episodes).
  \item \textbf{Per-stream tokenizers by density.} Dense force/torque
        ($\sim$100--125~Hz) is tokenized by a 1-D CNN over time (locally near-regular within a chunk), with the validity mask carried as extra channels so variable-DOF masking survives. Sparse streams (vision, joints at $\sim$10~Hz) stay as native tokens with continuous-time embeddings.
  \item \textbf{Multi-tick context and causality.} Context windows are selected
        by $\Delta t$ from cached timestamps; masking spans (modality $\times$ time) to predict held-out-time or future ee latents. Causality is an attention-mask property (causal for the prediction head and any streaming deployment; causal-vs-bidirectional tested for the representation objective), not baked into the tokenizer.
  \item \textbf{The decisive ablation.} Continuous-time (no resampling) vs.\
        resample-everything-then-1D-CNN (the classical DSP approach). This validates or kills the white-space claim; we build both and adopt the simpler one honestly if it wins.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Decoder (probe, not training signal)}
\label{sec:decoder}
A decoder is deliberately \emph{not} part of the training objective---training on reconstruction would drag the latent back toward pixel memorization (the autoencoder trap this design exists to avoid). Instead the decoder is a a generative probe: where a linear probe reports that information \emph{exists} in the latent, a generative decoder \emph{shows} what the latent knows. A cheap robot-state decoder on frozen latents quantifies content; a later diffusion-based pixel decoder samples plausible detail (decoding is one-to-many because the latent intentionally discards pixel nuisance) and serves as the product half of the compression story.

\paragraph{Timing model.}
The four nominally-10~Hz streams (joint, gripper, force/torque, TCP) are camera-aligned in the shipped data---per camera serial their timestamps are \emph{identical} to the color-frame timestamps---so the released data is not at native sensor rate (the raw high-frequency logs are). Effective tick rates are $\sim$6.7--14.7~Hz, varying by config and even by scene, with within-scene jitter. A separate high-frequency stream (wrist force/torque and TCP) is 100~Hz for cfg1 and 125~Hz for cfg2/3/4/6/7, absent for essentially all of cfg5 and empty for $\sim$$\tfrac{1}{3}$ of cfg3. Some scenes contain multi-minute recording gaps; we split each scene into contiguous segments at gaps $>500$~ms before chunking. These properties are the reason the model must accommodate irregular, multi-rate time rather than assume a fixed clock.

\paragraph{The chunk packet.}
Raw scenes are converted into tick-anchored chunks (chunk $k$ $=$ the $k$-th tick of a contiguous segment; native samples only, no interpolation). Each chunk is the robot-agnostic packet the model consumes (Table~\ref{tab:packet}): frozen ViT patch tokens, the masked $8\times3$ motor grid, the masked $13\times15$ end-effector block, a robot id, a tick timestamp, and index bookkeeping. The timestamp is cached per chunk specifically so the temporal phase needs no re-preprocessing. Chunks are precomputed to per-config caches on the NAS.

\paragraph{Split hygiene.}
The same user repeating the same task produces $\sim$10 near-duplicate scenes, so a frame- or scene-level split leaks near-copies of test scenes into training and inflates probes toward 1.0. We hold out by \textbf{(config, task, user) group}, stratified per config, frozen in a committed CSV (\texttt{holdout\_v1.csv}); all scenes of a group land on one side, and each config holds out $\sim$30\% of its groups. The split is generated from the raw scene listing, independent of which chunks were cached, and the dataloader errors on any group absent from the CSV. All evaluation is computed on the held-out groups only.

\paragraph{Camera choice.}
We use one deterministic \emph{external} camera per scene, excluding the wrist camera (identifiable per config from the rig's in-hand serials). Multi-view learning is deferred and, when added, will be framed as cross-view \emph{prediction}, never latent equality across views (the wrist-vs-side information-asymmetry objection is exactly what predict-don't-equate resolves).

\paragraph{Known data traps.}
Two documented traps bear on reproducibility: human-demonstration scenes come in both \texttt{\_human} and \texttt{\_human\_2} variants and a naive \texttt{endswith("\_human")} filter leaks the 543 \texttt{\_human\_2} scenes (harmless for video-only pretraining, but they carry no robot state and must be excluded from fusion training); and 57 robot scenes ($\sim$0.4\%) lack \texttt{joint.npy} and are skipped. The chunk pipeline filters both correctly.


\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup and evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:setup}
Our evaluation follows a standing rule (\S\ref{sec:stage1}): no single metric is reliable in isolation. Every evaluation therefore pairs a label-free health check with an unsaturated, robot-relevant probe, always group-held-out and multi-seed.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Linear probe $R^2$.} With the encoder frozen, fit a ridge probe
        from a feature to a held-out robot target (motor state, or force/torque$+$pose). $R^2\uparrow$: 1.0 perfect, 0 no better than the mean, negative worse than the mean. Unless noted, the evaluated feature is the \textbf{vision-only} fused latent $z_v$ (state masked at eval), so no state leaks in---the probe measures what the encoder \emph{learned to read out of pixels}.
  \item \textbf{RankMe (effective rank).} $\exp(\text{entropy of normalized
        singular values})$ of the latent~\citep{rankme2023}, a label-free collapse detector, bounded by $[1,\text{dim}]$. A collapsed latent can fake a good probe; RankMe catches it. It scales with dimensionality, so it is comparable only within the 256-d $z_v$ family, never against 768-d raw ViT features.
  \item \textbf{Baselines.} \emph{Raw vision}: mean-pooled frozen ViT features
        (768-d), the bar fusion must beat. \emph{PCA-256}: the top-256 principal directions of the frozen features---a pure compression control, fit on \emph{train rows only}. Beating PCA-256 isolates the gain as cross-modal learning rather than dimensionality reduction.
  \item \textbf{Geometry (planned).} Triplet accuracy over a hardest-first
        negative ladder (same-episode temporally-distant; same-task; same-config; different-config; and a modality-mismatch tier that cannot be solved from one modality), plus distance correlation and alignment/uniformity~\citep{alignuniform2020}, following the time-as-supervision precedent~\citep{tcn2018}. The computation is implemented; triplet \emph{selection} wiring is outstanding.
  \item \textbf{Protocol.} Group-held-out splits, 5 seeds, encoder retrained per
        split for the single-embodiment study and probes refit per split for the matrix; error bars are $\pm$std over seeds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Vision alone cannot see force}
\label{sec:stage1}
We first ask whether finetuning the vision backbone on robot video helps. It does not. Continuing LeJEPA on cfg3 video is a no-op: a hot learning rate (2e-4) collapses the encoder (RankMe $300\!\rightarrow\!158$); a gentle rate (2e-5) restores health (RankMe $\sim$285) but leaves robot-relevant probes flat (contact $0.69\!\rightarrow\!0.67$), and force $R^2\approx0$ for every checkpoint. The apparent drop in a task-id probe ($0.91\!\rightarrow\!0.74$) is a \emph{saturated} metric tracking ImageNet appearance, not a real regression---the first instance of the single-metric trap. The lasting finding is the motivation for the whole model: \textbf{a single frame does not contain force}, so vision alone cannot encode it. Hence the backbone is frozen (warm-started from the pretrained checkpoint) and force must enter through a different modality.

The probed feature is always the vision-only latent $z_v$ (state masked at eval), and we score two target groups separately: \emph{motor} (joints and gripper) and \emph{end-effector} (force/torque and TCP pose).

\paragraph{Findings.}
The results support three claims: fusion helps most where the camera is blind, one encoder covers all four robots, and the latent does not collapse.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Fusion wins where vision cannot see force.} On the
        end-effector probe (F/T $+$ pose), the ALL latent beats raw vision on every sensored robot ($+0.06$ flexiv, $+0.08$ ur5, $+0.04$ kuka; Table~\ref{tab:phase1diag}). This is the intended payoff: force does not appear in a single frame, so a latent trained alongside it recovers what pixels alone cannot.
  \item \textbf{Motor is a partial win, and the exception is informative.} The ALL
        latent beats raw on motor for flexiv, ur5, and franka, but trails it on kuka ($0.321$ vs.\ $0.391$). Instantaneous joint pose is already visible in the image, so fusion has little to add there. We expect the joint and motion payoff to arrive with the temporal extension (\S\ref{sec:temporal}), not at a single timestep.
  \item \textbf{One encoder covers all robots.} The ALL encoder matches or beats
        each specialist on that specialist's own robot (Table~\ref{tab:phase1diag}), while specialists transfer poorly: off its training robot, the franka specialist drops to $0.07$--$0.15$ and even the strong kuka specialist reads $0.11$--$0.22$ (Table~\ref{tab:phase1matrix}). Across the matrix, ALL sits at or near the best cell in every column. A specialist learned one robot; the ALL encoder learned robots.
  \item \textbf{Compression alone does not explain the gain.} PCA-256, the
        compression control, stays within $0.01$--$0.04$ of raw on every cell (Table~\ref{tab:phase1diag}), so reducing 768-d features to 256 dimensions is not what moves the probe. The cross-modal latent, the same width as PCA-256, is what beats raw.
  \item \textbf{The latent does not collapse.} RankMe of $z_v$ holds a healthy
        $\sim$165--180 band across robots (Table~\ref{tab:phase1rankme}), about two-thirds of the 256 ceiling. One ALL seed and one flexiv-specialist seed dipped (to $106$ and $66$), which widens those two error bars; the other four seeds of each sit near $178$--$190$. This is a per-seed training instability worth a re-run, not a failure of the recipe.
  \item \textbf{Franka is hard but stabilized.} Own-robot $R^2$ is negative for
        every method on cfg5, a small, force-blind config, yet the ALL latent ($-0.38$) is far more stable than raw's $-6.71$. Fusion regularizes the hardest embodiment even where it cannot make the probe positive.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the encoder learns to see: patch-feature PCA}
\label{sec:pca}
The transfer numbers say the fused latent carries robot-relevant structure; this section and the next ask \emph{how} the vision path is reshaped to carry it, using two standard qualitative probes. We stress that both are illustrative, not quantitative evidence: the claims below are read off representative held-out frames and are stated as interpretation.

We compare three per-patch feature spaces over the same test-split frame, reduced identically for a like-for-like read. Column~1 is the frozen pretrained ViT (LeJEPA ViT-B/16~\citep{lejepa2025}) patch tokens. Column~2 is the same frozen backbone with a single trained linear head (\emph{proj\_v}, $768\!\rightarrow\!256$; the vision-only baseline that shares the multimodal encoder's projection but sees no other modality). Column~3 is the Kepler encoder's own vision projection $\text{proj\_v}(\text{patch})+\text{mod}_v$---the last point in the pipeline where a spatial patch grid still exists, taken \emph{before} the Perceiver pools the patches into the non-spatial bottleneck, so it is the fair spatial comparison point. All three are projected to their top three principal components by a single joint PCA (robust 2--98 percentile normalization, bicubic upsampled to the image), following the DINO/DINOv2 patch-PCA visualization convention~\citep{dinov2_2023}. Figure~\ref{fig:pcacompare} shows the result across four embodiments.

The frozen ViT and the linear-head variant both render sharp, object-aligned regions: arm, workpiece, and background separate into distinct colors with crisp boundaries, the appearance ImageNet-pretrained ViTs are known for. The Kepler encoder's projection is visibly \emph{smoother}: the same principal-component colors bleed across patch boundaries and the object silhouettes soften. We read this as the spatial axes becoming more entangled: the per-patch code is no longer organized primarily by local appearance but redistributes information across the grid. This is the expected consequence of the training objective. Unlike a reconstruction loss, which rewards preserving patch-localized detail, the LeJEPA/SIGReg objective (\S\ref{sec:objective}) optimizes \emph{prediction of masked latents}, not pixels~\citep{ijepa2023}, and so has no pressure to keep the spatial code appearance-sharp; blurrier, more distributed patch features are consistent with a representation shaped for latent prediction rather than pixel-level fidelity. One confound tempers the visual comparison: the frozen-ViT panels are rendered at a finer patch grid ($28\!\times\!28$) than the encoder panel ($14\!\times\!14$, its trained token count), so part of the encoder's apparent smoothness is coarser-grid upsampling rather than feature entanglement. A matched-resolution rendering is left for the camera-ready.

\subsection{Where the encoder looks: attention maps}
\label{sec:attn}
The complementary question is spatial: where on the image does each model's attention land? We overlay three attention maps on the same frame sequence (Figure~\ref{fig:attncompare}). For the frozen pretrained ViT and a full-finetuned ViT (whole backbone trained on robot video), we take last-layer CLS-to-patch self-attention, averaged over heads (DINO-style~\citep{dinov2_2023}). For the Kepler encoder we take the Perceiver's final cross-attention---the $\text{softmax}(qk^\top)$ weight of its bottleneck queries over the 196 vision patches, averaged over heads and queries---i.e.\ what the fused bottleneck \emph{reads} from the image. We note the standard caveat that raw attention weight is not a faithful importance attribution~\citep{attnnotexpl2019}; we use these maps as an illustrative complement to the probe results, not as proof.

The pretrained ViT's attention is diffuse, spread broadly over the scene with the generic, texture-driven saliency untrained backbones default to. The Kepler encoder's cross-attention is markedly more concentrated: it collapses onto a small number of compact regions, most of them on or near the manipulated object and the gripper---the parts of the scene where the action is. Read alongside the transfer result, this is the qualitative face of the same finding: the bottleneck has learned to select task-relevant image regions rather than average over the frame.

A few activations fall on regions where nothing is visibly happening. We read these as the encoder \emph{anchoring} itself to the scene---attending to stable reference points (table edges, fixtures, the robot base) that fix the geometric frame, much as human gaze saccades to context-setting landmarks rather than fixating only on the point of action. This reading is a hypothesis, not a verified result: we have not run the diagnostics that would separate it from the known alternative in which ViTs park global information in a few high-norm background patches (register-token / attention-sink behavior~\citep[unverified]{registers2023}). A consistency test across scenes, a temporal replay checking whether these spots become active in later frames, and a patch-norm check are the natural next steps and are left as future work.

\paragraph{Bottleneck size and joint-SIGReg.} On ur5 the latent width has a sweet spot: $d{=}128$ gives $0.356/0.147$ (motor/ee), $d{=}256$ (the matrix setting) $0.324/0.156$, and $d{=}512$ degrades to $0.255/0.122$ with unstable RankMe (some seeds collapse), so $d{\in}[128,256]$ is preferred over a wider bottleneck. \subsection{Downstream usefulness on the frozen encoder}
\label{sec:downstream}
Beyond probes, we ask what the \emph{frozen} encoder is directly useful for, with no retraining. These results show the latent is rich and actionable; the fusion claim itself rests on the matrix and ablations above, not on the demonstrations here.

\paragraph{Invalid-state / safety detector.} Because the encoder is trained by cross-modal prediction, its per-sample prediction error is a built-in \emph{surprise} signal---low when the robot state is consistent with vision, high when it is not. On ur5 held-out, valid vs.\ corrupted state separates at AUROC $0.90$ (out-of-range state) and $0.69$ (state swapped from another scene), with no additional training: a robot-safety anomaly detector on the encoder we already have.

\paragraph{State read-out.} A small MLP decoding the frozen vision-only $z_v$ back to motor state reaches $R^2\,0.44$ vs.\ a linear probe's $0.335$ ($+0.11$)---the latent carries more state than a linear read-out exposes (per-dim: joint angle $0.69$). Gripper width is near-discrete, so as \emph{classification} $z_v$ gives $78\%$ accuracy / $0.87$ AUROC (raw ViT $77\%/0.86$). The latent also organizes by continuous world-state: within ur5, a PCA of $z_v$ traces a clean open$\rightarrow$closed gripper gradient (Figure~\ref{fig:pcagripper}), evidence it encodes graded state rather than only robot identity.

\paragraph{Pixel decode.} A small latent-conditioned diffusion decoder (PixNerd~\citep{pixnerd2026}) regenerates the camera frame from the frozen vision-only $z_v$ (Figure~\ref{fig:pixdecode}). Reconstructions are recognizable---scene layout, surfaces, colors, rough arm/object placement---but blurry by design: the 256-d latent keeps world-state and drops fine texture. Quality tracks scene complexity (clean on simple scenes, degrading on clutter). A mirror experiment decoding from a \emph{state-only} latent (vision hidden, motor$+$ee only) recovers arm pose from proprioception but not the scene (Figure~\ref{fig:crossdecode}). These are qualitative demonstrations of what the latent retains, not evidence for fusion.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Single timestep.} Every result here is instantaneous. The central
        bet---fusing multi-rate streams by real time without resampling (\S\ref{sec:temporal})---is designed and de-risked (the data carries per-token timestamps) but \emph{not yet validated}. The mixed motor result and the clean force result together predict the temporal phase is where the joint/motion payoff lands.
  \item \textbf{Correlational, not causal.} Same-time cross-modal alignment shows
        the latent \emph{contains} cross-modal structure, not that it models dynamics. Action-conditioned forward prediction (loss~4) is gated behind the temporal machinery.
  \item \textbf{Owed ablations.} The vision-only-trained control is the highest
        priority; until it is run, the cross-modal attribution rests on the PCA control alone (strong, but the two controls answer different objections).
  \item \textbf{Evaluation is probe-centric and objective-aligned.} Probing to the
        same state the model was trained to predict is informative but favorable; the planned triplet/geometry suite and the decoder probe are meant to stress the latent from angles the training loss does not.
  \item \textbf{One camera, single view.} Multi-view and wrist cameras are
        excluded for v1; adding them as cross-view prediction (not equality) is future work.
  \item \textbf{Data caveats.} RH20T's shipped streams are camera-aligned rather
        than native-rate, so the multi-rate claim is exercised against the raw high-frequency logs, not the released 10~Hz tick data; cfg5's force is a robot-internal estimate; and several positioning references are recent preprints.
\end{itemize}


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Kepler-Encoder-v1 is a robot-first multimodal encoder: it ingests video, proprioception, and force/torque as first-class \emph{(value, what, where, when)} tokens, fuses them with a Perceiver, and trains by masked cross-modal latent prediction under the LeJEPA/SIGReg objective---provably non-collapsing, prediction-based, and robust to missing sensors by construction. On RH20T we show that fusing sensors makes vision carry robot-relevant structure (a vision-only latent predicts state and, especially, force better than raw features and beats a compression control), and that a \emph{single} encoder covers four embodiments as well as per-robot specialists while transferring across them. The clean gains appear where the camera is blind. The next step is the model's defining claim: fusing streams across time at their native rates via continuous-time tokens---the white space that separates this encoder from synchronized-observation robot SSL.

\section{Single-embodiment fusion: cfg3 POC and cfg3\texorpdfstring{$+$}{+}cfg4 scale-up}
\label{sec:stage2}
Before scaling to the full transfer matrix (\S\ref{sec:phase1}), we fuse video $+$ robot state at a single timestep on UR5 data and ask whether the fused latent beats each modality alone, and whether the gain is \emph{cross-modal} rather than mere compression. A cross-modal signal gate confirms the premise (frozen vision $\rightarrow$ state $R^2=0.43$, TCP $0.73$), justifying the fusion encoder. We train the Perceiver with masked cross-modal latent prediction $+$ per-modal $+$ joint SIGReg, and at evaluation feed \emph{vision only}, probing to robot state. Table~\ref{tab:stage2} reports two runs: a cfg3 proof-of-concept ($\sim$24k frames) and a cfg3$+$cfg4 scale-up (86{,}430 frames / 2{,}881 scenes, $3.6\times$ the data).

  \label{tab:stage2}

$z_v$ beats raw vision by $+0.294\pm0.078$ (POC) / $+0.137\pm0.007$ (scale) and the PCA control by $+0.417\pm0.050$ / $+0.235\pm0.010$, positive on every seed. Two facts make this meaningful. First, \textbf{beating the PCA control shows the gain is cross-modal, not compression}: a latent $3\times$ smaller than raw vision predicts the robot \emph{better}, while PCA-256---the same compression with no cross-modal training---loses. Second, \textbf{only vision enters at eval}, so the encoder has \emph{learned to read robot-relevant structure out of pixels} by having been trained alongside state. This is exactly what a late-fusion recipe (train each modality's encoder separately, then concatenate) cannot produce: its vision features were never shaped by force or joints.

\paragraph{Why every baseline rises from cfg3 to cfg3$+$cfg4.}
The margin over raw narrows in absolute terms ($+0.294\!\rightarrow\!+0.137$) but stays decisive over the compression control ($+0.417\!\rightarrow\!+0.235$). The baselines rise largely for reasons that are \emph{not} ``raw vision got better at decoding within-robot state.'' (i)~$R^2$'s denominator changed: pooling two UR5 setups injects easy, pixel-readable \emph{between-config} variance (different mounts/backgrounds), which any encoder captures before decoding any pose. (ii)~The POC numbers are noisy/under-fit ($\pm0.075\!\rightarrow\!\pm0.010$, $7\times$ tighter). (iii)~PCA-256 improved the most ($+0.284$), diagnostic of the new between-config variance landing in its top components. (iv)~$z_v$ rose the least ($+0.102$)---it was already near saturation, which is why the margin over raw narrows while the margin over compression stays large. The comparison carrying the cross-modal claim is $z_v$ vs.\ the compression control, decisive in both runs.
