## TL;DR
- **Decision:** keep the encoder **per-frame**; put **time in a predictor**, not in the encoder.
- **Two builds now (both fully in our control, both on the shipped v0.1):**
  1. **Multi-camera** encoder — fuse N views into one latent (still single-timestep).
  2. **Next-embedding predictor** — a small model on the frozen encoder that predicts the future latent.
- **Then:** validate downstream (encoder-swap policy test) + action-condition the predictor.
- **Not doing:** another temporal-in-encoder attempt (retired); a bespoke Molmobot downstream test yet (parked).

## Why this shape (the decision)
The original v0.2 bet — fuse a *window of frames* into one latent (time inside the encoder) — **failed our
internal gate twice**: the fused latent was lower quality (effective rank 51 vs 134), predicted future force
*worse* than single-frame v0.1 (0.10 vs 0.17), and even halved the present-force signal. Root cause: pushing a
time-window through the fixed bottleneck dilutes the sharp per-frame signal. Evidence:
[RESULTS.md](results/temporal/RESULTS.md) §1–3; narrative: `TEMPORAL_JOURNAL.md` (git history).

So we move time **out** of the encoder into a predictor — the division of labor **JQ, FLARE, LeWorldModel, and
RoboTTT all converge on**. "Fixing v0.2 properly" and "doing FLARE" turn out to be the *same artifact*: a
next-embedding predictor on a frozen per-frame encoder. Temporal isn't dropped — it's relocated.

*Caveat (don't over-claim):* this is goal-dependent, not a law. V-JEPA 2 legitimately puts time in its encoder
because that encoder *is* the rollout world-model. Ours is a `g(·)` feeding a policy that already handles time,
so per-frame + predictor is the right split **for our downstream**. Ready answer if cited V-JEPA 2 back:
"their encoder is the rollout model; ours feeds a VLA that already handles time."

## Build 1 — Multi-camera on RH20T (`[B, 1, n_cam·196, 768]`)
Single-timestep, spatial-only (the `1` = one tick, explicitly no temporal). Plays to our validated strength
(v0.1 works at one frame; low architectural risk). Proves the Perceiver bottleneck can **compress N views**
without tanking latent quality, and it **saves the downstream policy context** (one compact latent instead of
`n_cam·196` raw patch tokens — JQ's ask).
- **RH20T first** because it has **both** multi-view *and* force/torque → validate the arch without losing the
  force story. Molmobot (5 cams, no force/torque) comes later for the downstream test.
- **Needs:** a per-view **camera-id embedding** (so views are distinguishable) + a **K-camera re-precompute**
  (current caches are single-view). Model side is near-free — Perceiver cross-attn is linear in token count.
- **Real risk:** more tokens into the *same* fixed latents = compression pressure → can rank-collapse. Fix =
  more latents (a known knob), but **measure it** — don't assume the bottleneck absorbs N views for free.
- **Pre-check B — DONE, GREEN (2026-07-17, `precheck_multicam.py`):** cross-view latent distance (0.665) ≈ **2×**
  cross-tick (0.335) on kuka → views are **complementary, not redundant** → multi-cam worth the precompute.
  (Measures that views differ; the compression test is Build 1 itself.) Numbers:
  [RESULTS.md](results/temporal/RESULTS.md) §5b.
- JQ's multi-cam file = `mm_perceiver3.py`. Detailed arch spec: `TEMPORAL_ARCH.md` §17 (git history) —
  but the build is DONE, so the real spec is the code (`train_multicam.py`, `mm_perceiver3.py`).
- **Build 1 trained — DONE, GATE GREEN (2026-07-19, `train_multicam.py`):** K=4 external cams, kuka
  (33.5k chunks, per-serial camera-id embedding, v0.1 recipe, ~30 min single GPU). On identical held-out
  chunks: **no rank collapse** (RankMe 153 vs v0.1's 136), **force-only R² 0.283 vs 0.251** (held AND
  improved — the probe the temporal design halved), pose 0.717 vs 0.635, and the same model fed 1 view drops
  to 0.132 force → the extra views genuinely pay. Caveat: the mc model is not a drop-in single-view encoder
  (1-view input is OOD for it; v0.1 remains the 1-cam model — camera-dropout training is the knob if needed).
  kuka/seed0 dev scope; matrix deferred to paper. Numbers: [RESULTS.md](results/temporal/RESULTS.md) §5c.
- **Composition analysis — DONE (2026-07-21, `multicam_composition.py`, JQ's Slack ask):** the fused embedding
  is **view-specific, not view-invariant** — different view-compositions of the *same instant* are 1.46× further
  apart than the same composition across *different instants* (partly by design: camera-id embeddings + views are
  complementary per pre-check B). Fine for fixed rigs; ~~camera-dropout training is the knob for missing-camera
  robustness~~ (falsified 07-21 — measured, it contracts the space instead; §5e(3)). And JQ's proposed
  late-fusion baseline (mean of per-view v0.1 embeddings) is **worse than a single
  v0.1 view** on every probe → **early fusion > single view > late fusion**; the Perceiver is doing real selection
  work, not a fancy mean. Numbers: [RESULTS.md](results/temporal/RESULTS.md) §5d.

  no view dominates; against the any-view hypothesis. Guardrail honored: info-retention probes ran alongside

## Build 2 — Next-embedding predictor (time, relocated)
Freeze the whole v0.1 encoder; train a **separate** small predictor to map `z_t → z_{t+Δ}`. This is (a) JQ's
"JEPA for the time dimension," (b) the temporal capability we wanted, (c) the de-risk for the FLARE integration.
- **Recipe (from LeWorldModel):** separate predictor, MSE in latent space, anti-collapse via **SIGReg on the
  marginal — NOT an EMA target** (SIGReg is already our method → simpler *and* a clean difference from
  FLARE/V-JEPA). Keep the v0.1 cross-modal head + joint-SIGReg.
- **Guardrails (so we don't recreate the v0.2 failure):**
  1. **Freeze the encoder** — training the encoder is what degraded v0.2; freezing makes that impossible. Joint
     fine-tune deferred.
  2. ~~Predict the latent *set*, not a mean-pooled vector~~ — **tested (2026-07-17): pooled ≥ set** for the
     per-frame predictor (the 8 spatial queries are redundant). The pooling that hurt v0.2 was over *ticks* (a
     time-window), not the encoder's per-frame spatial pooling — different thing. **Use pooled.**
  3. This is the **unconditioned** predictor; **loss #4** = this **+ action conditioning** (`ẑ_{t+1}=pred(z_t,a_t)`).
     Build the unconditioned one first, add actions after.
- **Pre-check A — DONE, GREEN (2026-07-17, `precheck_predict.py`):** a *simple* `z_t→z_{t+Δ}` predictor cuts
  latent-prediction error **~25–35% below carry-forward at every horizon**, consistent across kuka/flexiv/all;
  MLP ≈ linear (structure is ~linear at these horizons); the present latent recovers **future force** far above
  carrying it forward (kuka 0.28 vs 0.09). → **worth building.** Caveat: coarse cache (~1.7 s/tick) = this is
  *long-horizon* predictability; short-horizon needs a denser re-precompute. Numbers:
  [RESULTS.md](results/temporal/RESULTS.md) §4.
- **Build 2 trained — DONE, GREEN (2026-07-17, `train_predictor.py`):** the real artifact (frozen v0.1 → small
  horizon-conditioned predictor) beats carry-forward **~28–36% at every horizon** on kuka (seed0), reproducing
  the pre-check → not a probe artifact. **Decision: use the POOLED latent, not the set** — pooled ≥ set on every
  metric (MSE ratio 0.71/0.66/0.64 vs 0.72/0.67/0.65, higher R², RankMe 127 vs 100) at *half* the params; the
  8 spatial queries are redundant so predicting the set buys nothing here. Best-embodiment-1-seed for now; full
  embodiment×seed matrix deferred to paper. Numbers: [RESULTS.md](results/temporal/RESULTS.md) §5.

**Ordering note:** the two builds are **independent** — the predictor runs on *existing* data (`phase1` +
`caches/cfg*.npz`); multi-cam needs a new re-precompute. So order is a priority call: run/extend the predictor
while the multi-cam precompute runs in parallel.

## Next steps after the two builds — how the papers do it → our recipe
Two downstream paradigms in the literature: **(A) encoder feeds a policy that learns** (FLARE / behavior-cloning
swap) and **(B) predictor becomes a planner** (V-JEPA 2-AC). Our steps map onto them.

**N1 — Downstream encoder-swap test (the real validation; FLARE's ablation discipline).**
Fix one policy (behavior cloning / small diffusion policy), swap **only the observation encoder**
(raw ViT → SigLIP-2/DINOv2 → ours frozen → ours+predictor), measure **task success from rollouts** (not probe
R²). Mirrors FLARE's ablation (none 43.9% → raw 49.6% → pooled 50.9% → learned 55.0%). **Not gated on FLARE
code** — the fuller future-latent-auxiliary-loss version comes when GR00T coordination lands.
- **Molmobot = usefulness test only** (success flags + actions + language + 5 cams, but **no force/torque**) →
  it proves "the encoder helps a policy," not "force helps." Prefer **pretrain-on-Molmobot** (clean, no domain
  gap) over transfer-from-RH20T (real→sim confound). The force-in-downstream story stays an RH20T item.

**N2 — Action-conditioned predictor (world-model capability; LeWM + V-JEPA 2-AC). = loss #4.**
Extend Build 2 to `ẑ_{t+1}=pred(z_t, a_t)`, action injected via **AdaLN**, MSE + SIGReg (drop the EMA). Then use
it V-JEPA-2-AC-style as a **planner** (roll forward under candidate actions → MPC) for the eval.
- **First attempt (2026-07-17) — NO lift, parked.** Conditioned on the in-cache "action" (joint velocity dq) →
  no improvement (kuka: 0.643 vs 0.636), because dq is the *realized* velocity, **already encoded in z_t**. A
  meaningful loss #4 needs a *genuine* action: the **commanded** next-motion (not cached) or **DreamDojo-style
  latent actions from frame pairs** (the documented fallback for scarce action labels). RESULTS.md §5.
- **UNBLOCKED on RoboCasa + second NO-LIFT, sharper diagnosis (2026-07-20,
  `robocasa_predictor.py`, `results/temporal/robocasa_predictor_pnp.json`):** RoboCasa logs the
  **commanded** 12-D action per step → we ran `z_{t+Δ}` prediction (in-domain pretrained encoder, fused
  latent) with vs without the command sequence over the gap. Predictor beats carry-forward 11–19%
  (Build 2 replicates in-domain), but **commands add only 0.4–1.6%** (act/no-act 0.996/0.989/0.984 at
  0.25/0.5/0.75 s). Diagnosis upgrade: it's not just realized-vs-commanded — **in demonstration data the
  action is endogenous** (the operator acts on what they see: `a_t ≈ π(s_t)`), so commands carry little
  information beyond a state the latent already encodes. Consequences: (1) an action-conditioned
  world-model can't be *validated* on demos by in-distribution MSE — needs action-DIVERSE data
  (exploration/perturbations) or action-sensitivity evals (perturb the action input, check the prediction
  responds); (2) the faint trend (lift grows with horizon) says revisit at longer gaps; (3) still tell
  Alex to log commanded targets on the real rig — needed the day we collect anything non-demo.

**N3 — Denser re-precompute + write-up (EgoScale methodology).**
Report prediction loss as a cheap scaling proxy **but validate against real downstream success** (never one
metric). Re-precompute native-rate frames if fine dynamics (contact onset) are needed — Pre-check A was green
only at long horizons because the cache is coarse.

*One-line synthesis:* N1 = FLARE's encoder-swap discipline; N2 = LeWM's action-conditioned predictor with
SIGReg-not-EMA, used V-JEPA-2-AC-style as a planner; N3 = EgoScale's proxy-validated-against-success on denser
data. Biggest single borrow: **drop the EMA.**
